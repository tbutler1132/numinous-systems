# Notes: The Weakness of Cringe

## Core Argument

"Cringe" is essentially shame/guilt but redirected through the lens of others' perspective. That's why it's **weak**.

Shame and guilt are at least about oneself not living up to their own standards in relation to society—which is fine. People should seek to cooperate and be good members of their social group.

But cringe is literally just about how others perceive you, putting your self-worth into their hands.

---

## Unpacking the Mechanism

### Shame and Guilt (Legitimate Function)

- **Internal locus of evaluation**: "Did I meet my own standards?"
- **Social context acknowledged**: "Do my standards align with being a good community member?"
- **Evaluative authority retained**: You decide if you failed, based on principles you hold
- **Function**: Maintains cooperation, encourages self-correction, builds character
- **Direction**: Self → community (I evaluate myself in light of shared values)

### Cringe (Outsourced Evaluation)

- **External locus of evaluation**: "Will they think I'm weird/embarrassing/pathetic?"
- **No internal standard**: The only standard is "will I be judged negatively"
- **Evaluative authority surrendered**: They decide your worth
- **Dysfunction**: Paralyzes action, prevents sincere expression, makes you reactive
- **Direction**: Others → self (I evaluate myself as I imagine they evaluate me)

---

## Why Cringe is Weak

1. **No agency**: You've given the steering wheel to an imagined audience
2. **Preemptive**: You're not even responding to real judgment—you're responding to projected judgment
3. **Unfalsifiable**: Since it's imagined, you can never satisfy it. There's always someone who might cringe at you.
4. **Prevents growth**: Anything novel, sincere, or ambitious risks cringe. So you stay small.
5. **Scales infinitely**: With social media, the imagined audience is infinite. Cringe becomes paralyzing.

---

## The Contrast: When Shame/Guilt Work

**Scenario**: You snap at a friend unfairly.

- **Guilt response**: "I violated my own principle (treat friends with care). I need to apologize and do better."
  - Authority: internal (your principle)
  - Function: maintains relationship, self-improvement
  - Action: apologize, adjust behavior

- **Cringe response**: "They probably think I'm a terrible person now. Everyone will think I'm unstable. I'm so embarrassing."
  - Authority: external (their imagined judgment)
  - Function: self-flagellation, status anxiety
  - Action: spiral, avoid, or performatively overcompensate

One is functional. One is weak.

---

## The Cringe Industrial Complex

Modern digital culture weaponizes cringe:

- **Cringe compilations**: Public shaming for entertainment
- **Viral callouts**: Mistakes immortalized and spread
- **Parasocial evaluation**: Strangers judge you as if they know you
- **Quote-tweet dunks**: Performative cringe-calling for clout

This creates an environment where cringe becomes the dominant evaluative mode. And because cringe is weak, it produces:

- **Risk aversion**: Don't try anything that might fail publicly
- **Conformity**: Only do what's pre-approved
- **Inauthenticity**: Perform the "correct" opinions and aesthetics
- **Fragility**: Constant anxiety about being "cringe"

---

## Why This Matters for Creation

**You cannot create anything meaningful while governed by cringe.**

- All sincere expression risks being seen as cringe by someone
- All ambitious projects risk failure, which risks cringe
- All novel ideas risk misunderstanding, which risks cringe

If you let cringe rule you, you'll only ever do what's safe, pre-approved, and derivative.

---

## The Corrective

**Not**: "Don't care what anyone thinks" (sociopathic, also probably posturing)

**Instead**: "Evaluate yourself by your own principles, informed by the community you actually want to be part of"

This means:

1. **Have principles**: What do you actually value? What kind of person do you want to be?
2. **Choose your community**: Whose judgment actually matters to you? (Not randos on the internet)
3. **Accept risk**: If you're living by your principles, some people will think you're cringe. That's fine.
4. **Distinguish shame from cringe**: 
   - Shame: "I violated my principles" → useful signal, adjust course
   - Cringe: "Someone might judge me" → noise, ignore

## Connection to Other Ideas

### Beauty Redeems

- Redemptive moments (cathedral, holding your child, deep creation) require full presence
- Cringe makes presence impossible—you're trapped in the imagined gaze of others
- Beauty requires risk. All beauty risks cringe.

### Love of Fate

- Cringe is preemptive regret
- "If I do this, I'll regret how I'm perceived"
- Love of fate requires accepting that your actions will be misunderstood
- You must affirm them anyway

### The Living System

- Human viability includes capacity for self-evaluation
- Outsourcing that to others makes you:
  - **Fragile**: Your worth depends on their approval
  - **Reactive**: You can't lead, only follow
  - **Non-viable**: When the environment shifts, you have no internal compass

### Capital as Medium

- Building anything that matters requires tolerance for being "cringe"
- All new ideas seem cringe until they're normalized
- Orchestrating capital toward beauty means acting despite cringe risk

## Edge Cases and Nuances

**Q: What if you're actually being harmful and people call you out?**

A: That's not cringe—that's legitimate criticism. The question is: did you violate your own principles? If yes, adjust. If no, and you're confident in your principles, then it's noise.

**Q: Isn't some level of caring about others' opinions healthy?**

A: Yes—that's what shame and guilt are for. The question is: do you retain evaluative authority? Shame = you evaluate yourself with social context. Cringe = they evaluate you.

**Q: What about social norms and fitting in?**

A: Fitting in is fine if it aligns with your principles. But if you're contorting yourself purely to avoid judgment (cringe), you've surrendered agency.

- Compassionate but firm: "This is weak, and you deserve better"
